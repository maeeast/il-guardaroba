Il Guardaroba: Late 15th-Century Italian Dress

Extant-art picture dictionary and maker resource for late 15th-century Italian dress, headwear, foundation layers, and footwear.

Browse cards View citations Construction links

How to use this

Each card uses an image detail from period or near-period Italian art, with a citation link on the card. The images are visual evidence, not isolated museum photos of garments.

Underwear is the trickiest category because brache, women’s stockings, and some foundation layers are usually hidden in finished paintings. Those cards are marked with an evidence caveat instead of pretending the record is clearer than it is.

The Make it section links to tutorials, commercial patterns, project diaries, and videos. Those are construction aids, not period evidence.

Garments, hair, foundation layers, and shoes

Women’s garments Women Strong

Camicia

Lightweight linen underdress or shift worn next to the skin. In art, look for white linen at the neckline, cuffs, sleeve gaps, or beneath open sleeves.

Worn by
Women
Regions
All Italian regions

The period art often shows only the visible edges, because the camicia is the base layer under the dress.

View larger detail page

Citation: Domenico Ghirlandaio, Birth of Mary, Tornabuoni Chapel, Santa Maria Novella, Florence, 1486–1490. Public domain reproduction via Wikimedia Commons.

Women’s garments Women Strong

Gamurra

Main fitted dress with a shaped bodice and full skirt, worn over the camicia. It may lace at the front or side and often works with detachable sleeves.

Worn by
Women
Regions
Florence, Tuscany, northern and central Italy

A useful base dress term for late Quattrocento Florentine and north Italian impressions.

View larger detail page

Citation: Domenico Ghirlandaio, Giovanna Tornabuoni, c.1488, cropped image. Public domain reproduction via Wikimedia Commons.

Regional notes

Italy was not one fashion zone

Florence / TuscanyGhirlandaio’s Tornabuoni Chapel frescoes are the core visual anchor for gamurre, visible camicie, veils, formal robes, civic men, and elite women.
Venice / VenetoCarpaccio and Gentile Bellini give strong evidence for public Venetian dress, patrician robes, veils, and platform footwear.
Milan / LombardyThe Pala Sforzesca is a useful late-1490s anchor for Milanese court styling, especially women’s hair and headwear.
Ferrara / Este courtPalazzo Schifanoia gives a Ferrarese court context with Italian gamurre, detachable sleeves, fitted bodices, and high-waisted forms.
Mantua / Gonzaga courtUse Milan and Ferrara evidence cautiously for nearby court fashion; Mantua has its own court culture but overlaps in high-status styling.
Rome / Papal StatesUse central Italian forms cautiously: fitted dress, camicia, veils, robes, and clerical/civic garments, with strong influence from visiting artists and courts.
Naples / Aragonese SouthExpect stronger Spanish and Aragonese influence than Florence, especially in court fashion. The site flags this as regional caution rather than overclaiming.
Sicily / SardiniaVisual evidence is thinner for a quick costume page. Treat southern and island entries as Mediterranean/Aragonese-influenced rather than generic Florentine.
